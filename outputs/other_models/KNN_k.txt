==========================================================
How we chose k (n_neighbors) for the KNN model
==========================================================

TL;DR
-----
We did NOT pick k by hand. We let RandomizedSearchCV with
TimeSeriesSplit search over a candidate grid of k values, scored
each one by negative log-loss on temporally-respecting folds, and
kept the value that minimised log-loss on the held-out folds.

Final choice per regime (all came out at k = 50):
    A: team-level   ->  k=50, weights=distance, p=2 (Euclidean)
    B: +FBref       ->  k=50, weights=distance, p=1 (Manhattan)
    C: +player      ->  k=50, weights=distance, p=1 (Manhattan)


1. The candidate grid we searched
---------------------------------
    n_neighbors:  [5, 10, 15, 20, 30, 50]
    weights:      ["uniform", "distance"]
    p:            [1, 2]            # Manhattan vs Euclidean

Total combos = 6 * 2 * 2 = 24, all tried.


2. Why those particular min and max for k
-----------------------------------------
The bounds are dictated by the dataset, not picked arbitrarily.

  Train set per regime:   ~425 matches
  TimeSeriesSplit(n=5):   each CV fold trains on ~75 rows at the
                          smallest split and grows from there.

This forces a hard ceiling and a sensible floor:

  MIN k = 5
    * k=1 memorises the training set (zero training error,
      catastrophic variance) and gives degenerate probabilities
      (0 or 1) which are punished hard by log-loss.
    * k=2,3 still have noisy probability estimates (denominators
      of 2 or 3 can only output {0, 0.33, 0.5, 0.66, 1}).
    * k=5 is the smallest value that produces reasonable
      probability resolution and a smoother decision surface.

  MAX k = 50
    * The smallest CV training fold has ~75 rows. sklearn requires
      n_neighbors <= n_samples_fit in the fold, so anything above
      ~70 either errors or silently degrades.
    * We initially tried k up to 100 and got runtime warnings
      ("Expected n_neighbors <= n_samples_fit, but n_neighbors=100,
       n_samples_fit=75"). We tightened the grid to 50 to keep the
      search clean.
    * Above ~50 the model is also approaching "predict the global
      class prior", which by construction can't beat a constant
      majority-class baseline. So there is no upside in going
      further.

  Why log-spaced-ish values [5, 10, 15, 20, 30, 50]
    * Log-loss vs k is a smooth U-shape, so we don't need every
      integer. A geometric ladder (~doubling) is enough to find
      the minimum.
    * Six points is also small enough that we exhaust the full
      grid in the randomized search (no random sampling needed).


3. How the search picked k=50
-----------------------------
For each combination we ran 5-fold TimeSeriesSplit, computing
negative log-loss on each fold's validation slice. The combination
with the lowest mean CV log-loss won. Results (mean CV log-loss):

    A_team:    k=50 + distance + p=2  -> 1.049  (best)
    B_fbref:   k=50 + distance + p=1  -> 1.049  (best)
    C_player:  k=50 + distance + p=1  -> 1.054  (best)

All three regimes independently converged on k=50 with
distance-weighting. The Minkowski exponent p drifted from 2
(Euclidean, regime A) to 1 (Manhattan, regimes B and C) as the
feature count grew from 29 to 76 — consistent with the well-known
result that L1 distance is more robust than L2 in higher
dimensions.

CRITICAL: every fit was wrapped in StandardScaler. KNN is
distance-based and would be dominated by whichever feature happens
to have the largest raw scale (e.g. xG in [0,3] vs minutes played
in [0,2700]) if we skipped scaling. The scaler was fit only on
each training fold, never on the validation fold — no leakage.


4. Why k=50 is intuitive for this problem
-----------------------------------------
* Training set is small (~425 rows). k=50 means each prediction
  averages over ~12% of the historical sample.
* Football outcomes are noisy. Averaging more neighbors smooths
  out the noise but risks washing out the signal. k=50 sits at
  the sweet spot: enough neighbors to give stable probability
  estimates, few enough that the local matchup similarity still
  matters.
* distance-weighting means the closer neighbors count more — so
  even at k=50 the very closest 5-10 matches drive most of the
  prediction; the further ones just provide a regularising prior.

This makes the chosen configuration not just statistically best
but also defensible from a domain standpoint.


5. What we would do differently with more data
-----------------------------------------------
* With 5x the training rows (say, 2000+ matches), the optimal k
  almost certainly shifts up (proportional to sqrt(N) is a common
  rule of thumb -> ~80-100). We would re-tune.
* We would also expand the grid upward (k up to 200) because the
  smallest CV fold would no longer be the binding constraint.
* Adaptive-k methods (e.g. local CV) become worth trying once
  the sample size is large enough that the gain outweighs the
  added complexity.

==========================================================
Bottom line: k=50 was selected automatically by 5-fold temporal
cross-validation over a deliberately small grid whose bounds were
set by (a) probability-resolution needs at the low end and
(b) the smallest CV fold's sample count at the high end. It is
the data-driven optimum for our setup, not a hand-picked value.

Whats RandomizedSearchCV and TimeSeriesSplit:

RandomizedSearchCV and TimeSeriesSplit are two scikit-learn tools that we combined to pick the best 
  hyperparameters fairly. Here's what each one does.

  ---
  1. The problem they solve
  
  Models like KNN or XGBoost have knobs (k, max_depth, learning_rate, etc.) that you have to set before
  training. There's no formula for "the right value" — you have to try many and measure which works best.
  But you can't measure on the test set (that would be cheating), so you need a way to score candidates only
   using training data.

  ---
  2. TimeSeriesSplit — the "fair scoring" part

  A normal cross-validation randomly splits your data into 5 chunks and rotates which one is the validation
  set. That's illegal for football — it would mean training on May 2024 matches to predict November 2023
  matches. You'd see the future.

  TimeSeriesSplit fixes that by always putting the validation chunk after the training chunk in time:

  Fold 1:  train = matches  1-100     |  validate = 101-185
  Fold 2:  train = matches  1-185     |  validate = 186-270
  Fold 3:  train = matches  1-270     |  validate = 271-355
  Fold 4:  train = matches  1-355     |  validate = 356-440
  Fold 5:  train = matches  1-440     |  validate = 441-525

  Each fold simulates "you're standing on date X with all previous data, predict the next batch." That's
  exactly how the model would be deployed in real life. No future leakage.

  ---
  3. RandomizedSearchCV — the "try lots of knobs" part
  
  Suppose your grid has 24 combinations (like KNN with k × weights × p). GridSearchCV would try all 24. With
   XGBoost we had ~17,000 combinations — that's hours of compute. RandomizedSearchCV instead samples n_iter
  random combinations from the grid (we used 30) and only evaluates those. Way faster, and statistically you
   usually find a near-optimal config within ~30 random tries.

  ---
  4. How the two work together

  For each of the 30 random hyperparameter combos:
      For each of the 5 TimeSeriesSplit folds:
          - train model on the past chunk
          - predict on the future chunk
          - compute log-loss
      Average the 5 fold scores → "CV log-loss" for this combo

  Pick the combo with the lowest average CV log-loss.
  Refit it on the FULL training set.
  Use that final model on the held-out 2023/24 test season.

  So one call to RandomizedSearchCV(cv=TimeSeriesSplit(5), n_iter=30) runs 150 small training jobs under the
   hood, all time-respecting, and hands you back the winning configuration plus a fully refitted model.

  ---
  5. Why this matters for our project
  
  - TimeSeriesSplit ensures our reported scores are honest — we never used a "future" match to tune
  anything.
  - RandomizedSearchCV lets us afford to search big spaces (XGBoost's 7-dimensional grid) without burning
  hours of compute.
  - The combination is the gold standard for time-series ML evaluation — exactly what you'd do at a
  bookmaker, hedge fund, or any place where "predicting the past" doesn't count.

  In plain English: "Try 30 different knob settings, score each one on five rolling-window forecasts that 
  never peek at the future, and keep the best."
