Highlights
- Player-level rolling statistics add real predictive signal beyond team form and bookmaker odds.
- **Feature selection** supports the result: `net_pl_starter_mins` and `net_pl_pct_fw` rank highly across all four selection methods, and LASSO zeroes 0 player features out of 24.
- **Tree models** show the player signal most clearly: every tree model improves once player features are added, while logistic regression gets worse — it struggles more with correlated, high-dimensional inputs.
- Including **Understat-derived features** (shot-level xG aggregates) produced the clearest jump in predictive performance, and they dominate the **feature-importance** rankings of Random Forest and XGBoost.
- The best result is the **RF + XGBoost ensemble** with **isotonic calibration** on the full player-feature regime: log-loss 1.04, accuracy 50.6%.
- KNN is a notable surprise: with k=50 and distance weighting it nearly matches the ensemble, suggesting that averaging similar past matches works well on a small, noisy dataset.
- Six different algorithms converge within ~0.03 log-loss of each other — evidence of an **information ceiling** in public historical stats. The full pipeline is reproducible from public data and closes about a third of the uniform-to-Understat gap.

Brief Report

Scope. The goal of our project is to predict Ligue 1 match outcomes (home win, draw, away win) using team- and player-level data over seasons 2018–2024, and to test whether **disaggregating to individual player statistics** adds predictive power beyond team aggregates and the bookmaker baseline. We train on 2018–2022 and hold out the 2023/24 season for testing throughout.

Data. We built the dataset from three independent public sources: Understat (xG timelines, results, pre-match win/draw/loss probabilities — 2,105 matches), Transfermarkt (squad market value, average player value, squad age — 174 team-seasons), and FBref (per-player per-match stats: goals, assists, shots, SoT, tackles, interceptions, minutes, position — 62,683 player-match rows). FBref was scraped via the `soccerdata` package with an undetected headless Chrome driver; the 2021/22 season was initially blocked by a CAPTCHA and required a visible-browser pass to recover. Joining on date and team name gave a 2,092-match table with 87 columns; dropping cold-start rows (first ~3 matchdays per season, where rolling windows are not yet filled) left a clean 1,653-match dataset with 73 candidate features across 29 teams.

Feature engineering. Three stages. Stage 1 — Understat + Transfermarkt: **rolling 5-match** means of xG, goals, wins, points per team, plus squad market value, average player value, squad age, and bookmaker implied probabilities. Stage 2 — FBref team-level: rolling possession, formation (attackers count), and player season G+A/90 aggregated to team level. Stage 3, our novel contribution — player-level: per-player rolling shots, SoT, defensive actions (TklW + Int), goals and assists **per 90 minutes**, computed *individually* so a mid-season transfer does not reset a player's form, then aggregated over the starting XI as a **minutes-weighted mean**. Each player feature exists as `home_pl_*`, `away_pl_*` and `net_pl_*` (home − away) variants; we also add starter-minutes regularity, forward share (`pct_fw`), and depth-gap proxies. All rolling windows use `shift(1)` before the mean so the current match is never used to predict itself.

Feature selection. Each of the 73 features was scored by **four independent methods**, percentile-normalised to [0, 1], and averaged into a composite leaderboard: (1) **Mutual Information** as a non-parametric filter; (2) **RFECV** with a Random Forest under **temporal cross-validation** as a wrapper (optimal subset: 26 features); (3) **LASSO** logistic regression as an embedded method (17 features zeroed, but **0 player features zeroed**); (4) **Random Forest mean-decrease-in-impurity** as a second embedded method. Two player features enter the top 11: `net_pl_starter_mins` (rank 9) and `net_pl_pct_fw` (rank 11); ten player features survive RFECV. Heatmaps, scatterplots, and distribution plots confirmed that player features are **near-zero correlated** with the bookmaker signal — they carry new information rather than re-encoding the odds — while also flagging strong **multicollinearity** between teammates and between related metrics (xG / shots / SoT).

Baseline and the negative LR result. To get a first baseline we trained a **multinomial logistic regression** on team-level features. We used a strict temporal split (train 2018–2022, test 2023/24) to avoid leaking the future into the past, and applied a `StandardScaler`. The Understat win probabilities were excluded from the model since they serve as our external baseline: using them as both input and reference would be circular. We then ran the same model on three feature regimes — (A) team-only, (B) +FBref team-level, (C) +player — on a common subset of 593 matches with valid data across all three CSVs. The log-loss actually got worse as we added more features (1.06 → 1.14 → 1.22). We attribute this to **multicollinearity** between team- and player-level features and the **curse of dimensionality** (417 training matches for up to 76 features). This negative result motivated the move to tree-based models, which handle correlated features natively.

Tree models. We trained **Random Forest** and **XGBoost** on the same three regimes (A/B/C) and the same temporal holdout, tuned with **RandomizedSearchCV** (up to 100 configurations) over **TimeSeriesSplit(5)** so no validation fold ever contains a match earlier than its training fold. **Draw class imbalance** was handled via class weights (RF) and sample weights (XGB). Each fitted model was then wrapped in `CalibratedClassifierCV` with **isotonic calibration** on a temporal inner split. The two calibrated models were combined into a 50/50 **RF + XGBoost ensemble** for the final prediction. Trees reverse the LR story: RF improves 1.257 → 1.059 → 1.054 from regime A to C, XGBoost stabilises at 1.053 in C, and the ensemble is best overall at **1.042 log-loss** with 50.6% accuracy. **Feature-importance rankings for both RF and XGBoost are dominated by Understat-derived team-form features** (rolling xG, market value), with `net_pl_starter_mins`, `net_pl_pct_fw`, and depth-gap features appearing repeatedly — consistent with the feature-selection leaderboard.

Other models and the information ceiling. Under the same protocol we also fit four additional model families: a **polynomial** logistic expansion (degree 2), **Ridge (L2)** logistic, **Lasso (L1)** logistic, **KNN** (grid over k ∈ {5, 10, 15, 20, 30, 50} × {uniform, distance} × {Manhattan, Euclidean}), and a single **decision tree** as a control. Polynomial overfits catastrophically (1.50 log-loss in regime C, 400+ inputs from a degree-2 expansion). Ridge and Lasso are essentially tied (regularisation type does not matter much here). KNN independently picks k = 50 with distance weighting in every regime and reaches 1.01–1.02 — tying the tree ensemble. A single unregularised decision tree is the worst model in every regime (≥ 1.96), confirming the value of bagging and boosting. Six families (Ridge, Lasso, KNN, RF, XGB, ensemble) end up within **~0.03 log-loss** of each other in regime C — evidence of an **information ceiling** on what public historical aggregates can deliver. No model beats the **Understat bookmaker baseline** of 0.910, which prices in real-time information (injuries, lineups, team news) absent from our features; our best model closes ~33% of the uniform-to-Understat gap (1.099 → 0.910).

Evaluation. Primary metric is **log-loss**, with accuracy as a secondary reference (random guess 33%, always-home 43%, our best model 50.6%, bookmaker 56%). All metrics are computed on the held-out 2023/24 season; hyperparameters are chosen on TimeSeriesSplit(5) inside the training window only. A **regime comparison** (A/B/C) on the same 593-match subset checks whether the player-level advantage holds when the feature set changes.

What was left out. We did not include lineup-confirmation features (known only close to kickoff), in-game live state, referee or weather data, or an explicit Elo baseline. Deep sequence models (LSTM/transformers over match sequences) were not pursued: with six algorithms already converging, the bottleneck is **data**, not model capacity. Per-season ablations and an Elo comparison would be natural next steps.

Main references. Course material on logistic regression, regularisation, tree ensembles, calibration, and time-series cross-validation. Data sources: FBref, Understat, Transfermarkt. Tooling: scikit-learn, XGBoost, soccerdata.
